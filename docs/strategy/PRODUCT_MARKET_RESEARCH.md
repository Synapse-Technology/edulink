# EduLink Product-Market Research Brief

Date: 2026-05-02

## Core Question

EduLink should not be positioned as "another internship/job board." Kenya already has job boards, government portals, institutional attachment systems, WhatsApp circulation, LinkedIn, Fuzu, BrighterMonday, NEAIMS, Kenya Skills Hub, NITA/ITAP, and institution-specific workflows.

The stronger product thesis is:

> EduLink is a trusted work-based learning operating system for Kenyan institutions, students, and employers, focused on verified student identity, structured attachment/internship workflows, employer trust, supervision, evidence, reporting, and post-placement outcomes.

That is materially different from a generic job marketplace.

## What The Current Product Has Right

- Multi-sided roles are already present: student, employer, institution, supervisor, admin.
- The product already understands the real lifecycle: application, verification, placement, logbook, evidence, supervisor feedback, completion, certificate/artifact verification.
- Trust mechanics exist: affiliation verification, document upload, employer verification, trust tiers, application rate limits.
- Institution authority has been considered: departments/cohorts should be canonical and institution-owned, not student-created.
- This creates a possible moat: verified education-to-work records, not just resumes.

## What Is Wrong Or Under-Validated

- The current product appears feature-led, not wedge-led. It has many modules, but no sharply proven first buyer.
- Student features are overrepresented relative to the hard go-to-market problem: institutions and employers must participate or students will churn.
- There is no explicit answer yet to "who pays?"
- Messaging, status transparency, and feedback are still weaker than the core trust promise requires.
- The public story risks sounding like a job board unless the institutional workflow is made central.
- There is no evidence yet that employers will post opportunities directly without institutional pressure, incentives, or managed support.

## Kenyan Market Reality

Kenya's internship and attachment market is fragmented. Students find opportunities through job boards, LinkedIn, employer websites, school offices, WhatsApp groups, government portals, personal networks, and direct email. That fragmentation is the pain, but it also means distribution is the hard part.

Relevant signals:

- Kenya Skills Hub describes itself as a one-stop virtual hub for youth access to skills, work-based learning, internships, career guidance, and employment opportunities. It reports 13,777,600 youth supported with information and 254,312 job opportunities on NEAIMS.
- NEAIMS publicly shows jobseekers, employers, fake recruitment agency reporting, and legitimacy checks. That confirms trust and fraud are not side issues in Kenya's employment market.
- NITA's industrial attachment function explicitly links industry and institutions for workplace placement and practical skills acquisition.
- Public TVET enrollment is growing strongly. The Ministry of Education's TVET sub-sector reporting shows public TVET enrollment under MoE rising from 345,387 in FY 2022/23 to 565,842 in FY 2024/25.
- Job scams are active enough that Kenyan agencies and media repeatedly warn applicants about fake recruitment links, fake SMS messages, and fee-based fraud.

Implication: EduLink should compete on verified workflow and institutional trust, not on having "more listings."

## Comparable Brands And Lessons

### LinkedIn

LinkedIn's moat is the professional graph: people, companies, jobs, schools, and skills. Its Economic Graph claims over 1.2B members, 41,000 skills, 69M companies, and 140,000 schools. LinkedIn also keeps investing in verification because trust is now a core hiring problem, not just a profile feature.

Lesson for EduLink:

- Do not copy LinkedIn's broad professional networking. Copy the graph logic.
- Build a Kenyan education-to-work graph: verified students, institutions, departments, cohorts, supervisors, employers, opportunities, competencies, logbooks, evidence, completions, outcomes.
- Trust should be visible in the product, not hidden in admin logic.

### Handshake

Handshake's strongest idea is institution-mediated early-career recruiting. Students enter through universities; employers get access to verified student/alumni pools; career centers get reporting and engagement tools. Public university career pages describe Handshake as connecting students/alumni with employers, supporting job posts, applications, events, advising, and employer engagement.

Lesson for EduLink:

- The institution is not just another user role. It may be the best distribution channel and buyer.
- Career services/attachment offices need workflow, reporting, approval, supervisor assignment, and placement outcomes.
- Students should be acquired through cohorts, not one by one through ads.

### Fuzu

Fuzu competes strongly in Kenya and East Africa as a career development and talent matching platform. It advertises AI matching, profile/CV builder, career guidance, employer tools, assessments, candidate ranking, branded career pages, 3M+ talent profiles, 120k+ jobs posted yearly, and 16M+ applications submitted.

Lesson for EduLink:

- Do not fight Fuzu head-on as a general job board or AI matching platform.
- Fuzu owns broad career marketplace language. EduLink should own verified student attachment/internship operations and institutional reporting.
- Employer value must include time saved: pre-verified students, structured documents, institution-backed eligibility, supervisor flow, compliance trail.

### BrighterMonday

BrighterMonday is positioned as a broad recruitment and career platform in Kenya, with verified employers, job seeker alerts, career support, employer screening, and HR services.

Lesson for EduLink:

- Generic job discovery is crowded.
- EduLink's wedge must be narrower and deeper: internships, attachments, learning outcomes, institution-employer coordination.

### Kenya Skills Hub, NEAIMS, NITA

These are public-sector adjacent systems around jobs, skills, work-based learning, industrial attachment, and employment information.

Lesson for EduLink:

- Government already validates the category.
- EduLink should integrate with, reference, or complement these flows instead of pretending they do not exist.
- The opportunity is operational execution, data cleanliness, and trust UX for institutions and employers.

## Best Initial Customer Segment

The best first wedge is probably not "all Kenyan students."

Better first beachhead:

> Private universities, TVETs, and career/attachment offices that need to place, supervise, verify, and report on student attachments/internships.

Why:

- They own student cohorts.
- They feel the administrative pain.
- They care about employability metrics and employer relationships.
- They can mandate or strongly encourage student onboarding.
- They benefit from reports by department, cohort, employer, completion, supervisor load, and outcomes.

Secondary segment:

> SMEs and mid-sized employers willing to host interns if candidate quality, paperwork, and supervision are simplified.

Student acquisition should follow the institution and employer supply, not lead it.

## Business Model Options

Most plausible:

- Institution SaaS: annual subscription per institution, priced by active students/cohorts.
- Employer subscription: paid only after there is enough verified student supply; offer free early posting for trusted pilot employers.
- Managed placement fee: high-touch placement and screening for employers that do not want to manage applications.
- Verification/certificate revenue: charge institutions or employers, not students initially.

Avoid early:

- Charging students for access. It weakens trust and adoption.
- Competing on paid job posts before there is enough candidate liquidity.
- Building a broad social feed. It distracts from the workflow moat.

## Product Strategy

EduLink should become trust-first and workflow-first.

Priority product pillars:

1. Verified identity and affiliation
   - Student belongs to institution, department, cohort.
   - Institution controls canonical academic structure.
   - Raw student claims are preserved but not treated as truth.

2. Verified employer and opportunity quality
   - Employer profile verification.
   - Public trust signals.
   - No fee-charging opportunities unless explicitly verified and disclosed.
   - Report suspicious opportunity flow.

3. Placement workflow
   - Opportunity creation.
   - Eligibility rules.
   - Applications.
   - Review.
   - Interview/selection status.
   - Institution approval where required.

4. Communication and transparency
   - In-app threads between student, employer, supervisor, institution.
   - Status explanations.
   - Rejection/feedback reasons where possible.
   - Required document reminders.

5. Supervision, evidence, and completion
   - Logbooks.
   - Milestones.
   - Artifacts.
   - Supervisor feedback.
   - Completion certificate.
   - Public verification link.

6. Reporting and outcomes
   - Placement rate by cohort.
   - Completion rate.
   - Employer satisfaction.
   - Supervisor load.
   - Skills gained.
   - Time-to-placement.

## Go-To-Market

Recommended pilot:

- Pick 1 institution, 1 department, 1 cohort, and 10-20 employers.
- Target 100-300 students, not the whole market.
- Run a full attachment cycle from verification to completion.
- Keep employer posting mostly assisted at first.
- Measure placement, completion, response times, and admin hours saved.

Acquisition motion:

- Institution-first sales: dean, career services, attachment coordinator, department heads.
- Employer partnership: SMEs, NGOs, county-linked businesses, hospitals, ICT firms, banks, manufacturing, hospitality.
- Student onboarding through institution cohorts.
- Trust marketing: verified employers, no fake listings, institution-backed records, no student fees for applications.

## Metrics That Matter

Do not optimize for registrations first.

Primary metrics:

- Active verified students per cohort.
- Verified employers with at least one approved opportunity.
- Application-to-shortlist rate.
- Shortlist-to-placement rate.
- Placement-to-completion rate.
- Median employer response time.
- Median institution verification time.
- Admin hours saved per cohort.
- Percentage of opportunities with complete trust profile.
- Number of completed verifiable artifacts/certificates.

## Main Risks

- Cold start: students without employers or employers without students.
- Institution bureaucracy: slow approvals and unclear ownership.
- Employer apathy: SMEs may like interns but not want another dashboard.
- Trust failure: one scam listing can damage the brand.
- Feature sprawl: building more dashboards before proving one repeatable workflow.
- Marketplace liquidity: broad listings require scale; workflow SaaS requires depth.

## Recommended Next 30 Days

1. Stop broad feature expansion unless it directly supports the pilot workflow.
2. Interview:
   - 10 students who recently searched for attachment/internship.
   - 5 attachment/career office staff.
   - 10 employers that have hosted or rejected interns.
   - 3 supervisors/lecturers.
3. Validate willingness to pay with institutions, not students.
4. Build a pilot package:
   - Student verification.
   - Employer verification.
   - Opportunity posting.
   - Application review.
   - Messaging/status.
   - Logbook/evidence.
   - Completion report.
5. Remove or hide features that are not needed for this pilot.
6. Create a trust policy:
   - Employer verification rules.
   - Opportunity approval rules.
   - No-fee rule.
   - Scam reporting.
   - Data protection and consent.

## Hard Product Positioning

Bad positioning:

> EduLink helps students find internships.

Stronger positioning:

> EduLink helps Kenyan institutions run trusted student attachment and internship programs with verified employers, structured supervision, evidence, and outcome reporting.

Student-facing version:

> Find verified attachment and internship opportunities through your institution, track your progress, submit logbooks, and leave with proof of real work experience.

Employer-facing version:

> Access institution-verified students, manage applications, supervise interns, and reduce the paperwork of work-based learning.
